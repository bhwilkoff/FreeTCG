# TCG Games Overview

A reference catalog of eleven trading and deck-building card games, focused on the structural elements a designer needs to know before pitching a new TCG. Each entry covers resource system, turn structure, win condition, card types, deck construction, and a distinctive-design summary.

*Source caveat: assembled from training-data knowledge (cutoff Jan 2026); URLs marked "(unverified URL — recall from training, not freshly fetched)" are pointers for further reading, not citations.*

---

## 1. Magic: The Gathering (Wizards of the Coast, 1993)

**Resource system.** Mana, generated by tapping land cards. Five colors (white, blue, black, red, green) plus colorless. Players draw lands from the same 60-card deck as their spells, which means resource development is itself a probabilistic gamble — the canonical "mana screw / mana flood" problem. A typical deck runs 22-26 lands.

**Turn structure.** Five phases (beginning, main 1, combat, main 2, end), with combat broken into declare-attackers, declare-blockers, damage. The defining feature is the **stack**: when a player casts a spell, the opponent can respond before it resolves, producing nested last-in-first-out resolution. Priority passes back and forth; instants and abilities can be played on any player's turn.

**Win condition.** Reduce opponent's life total (starts at 20) to 0, or force a deck-out (drawing from an empty library). A handful of "alternate win conditions" exist on specific cards (Approach of the Second Sun, Mill).

**Card type taxonomy.** Lands, creatures, artifacts, enchantments, planeswalkers, instants, sorceries, battles (recent), tribal/kindred (legacy). Sub-classification by type, subtype, supertype.

**Deck size and construction.** Standard/Modern: 60-card minimum (no maximum), max 4 of any non-basic card. Commander: exactly 100 cards, singleton, color-identity locked to a legendary commander.

**What makes it distinctive.** The mana system, the stack, and the **color pie** are MTG's three load-bearing inventions. Mark Rosewater discusses the color pie extensively on his *Drive to Work* podcast (unverified URL — recall from training, not freshly fetched: blogatog.com / magic.wizards.com/en/news/columns). Each color has a sanctioned philosophy and mechanical toolkit, and breaking the pie is an explicit design taboo. Rosewater's "New World Order" essay also reframed common-rarity card design around readability for new players.

## 2. Pokémon TCG (Wizards/The Pokémon Company, 1996)

**Resource system.** Energy cards, attached one per turn from hand. There are nine basic energy types matching the Pokémon types. Special energy cards provide multiple types or extra effects. Energy stays on a Pokémon until it's knocked out or returned.

**Turn structure.** Draw, then any combination of: play Basic Pokémon, evolve, attach one energy, play Trainer cards, retreat (paying energy), attack (ends turn). Single attack per turn. No interactive responses on the opponent's turn — turns are largely sequential.

**Win condition.** Take all your prize cards (6 in standard, fewer in shorter variants). You take a prize whenever you knock out an opposing Pokémon. Also win by knocking out an opponent's last Pokémon in play, or if they cannot draw at start of turn.

**Card type taxonomy.** Pokémon (Basic, Stage 1, Stage 2, with subtypes V, ex, GX, VMAX across eras), Energy (Basic, Special), Trainers (Item, Supporter — one Supporter per turn — Stadium, Tool).

**Deck size and construction.** 60 cards exactly. Max 4 of any card except basic Energy (unlimited).

**What makes it distinctive.** The **prize-card system** is a genius pacing mechanism: it doubles as a win-condition tracker and a hand-replenisher (you literally draw your prize when you score). Evolution chains create deck-construction puzzles where you need both the Basic and the Stage cards in correct ratios. Energy attachment caps tempo (one per turn) so big attackers feel earned.

## 3. Hearthstone (Blizzard, 2014)

**Resource system.** Mana crystals that auto-increment. You start with 1 crystal on turn 1, gain 1 each turn up to a cap of 10. There is no mana resource card to draw — every turn always has the "right" amount of mana.

**Turn structure.** Strict turn-based with no opponent-turn interaction (with rare exceptions like Secrets and Counterspell). Draw, play cards, attack with minions/hero, end turn. Single-threaded.

**Win condition.** Reduce opponent's hero from 30 health to 0. Fatigue (cumulative damage when drawing from an empty deck) acts as a soft secondary win condition.

**Card type taxonomy.** Minions, Spells, Weapons (equipped to hero), Hero cards (replacing the default hero mid-game in later sets), Locations (recent). Each of nine classes has its own card pool plus shared "Neutral" cards. Each hero has a unique **hero power**, costing 2 mana, usable once per turn.

**Deck size and construction.** 30 cards, max 2 of any card (1 of a Legendary). Class-locked plus Neutrals.

**What makes it distinctive.** Hearthstone's design philosophy, often discussed in interviews with Ben Brode (e.g., on the *Joe Rogan Experience* and various GDC talks — unverified recall), prioritizes **streamlining**: removing instant-speed interaction, removing land cards, and embracing **RNG** (Yogg-Saron, Discover, random draws) as a feature rather than a bug. The justification: digital play lets the computer adjudicate complex random effects, and randomness creates memorable moments that make for shareable streams.

## 4. Marvel Snap (Second Dinner, 2022)

**Resource system.** Auto-incrementing energy, like Hearthstone, but capped at 6. Turn 1 = 1 energy, Turn 6 = 6 energy. Game is exactly 6 turns long.

**Turn structure.** Both players plan secretly and reveal **simultaneously**. A priority system (the player who is winning reveals first) breaks ties. After turn 6, scores are compared. Speed is the headline: average match is 3 minutes.

**Win condition.** Win 2 of 3 **locations**. Each location has a special rule (revealed across turns 1-3) and counts power totals of cards played there. Also: the **Snap/Cube** mechanic — at any point a player can "snap" to double the cube wager, retreating costs you only 1 cube while playing through to a loss costs you the snapped amount.

**Card type taxonomy.** A single card type (cards with cost and power), but with rich keywords: On Reveal, Ongoing, Discard, Destroy, Move, etc.

**Deck size and construction.** Exactly 12 cards. No duplicates (singleton).

**What makes it distinctive.** Ben Brode (post-Hearthstone) intentionally **designed for matchmaking time, not just match time**. The Snap/Cube system is the conceptual heart: ladder rank is measured in cubes, not wins, so retreating is correct play more often than you'd expect — a real innovation in incentive design. The 12-card deck makes deckbuilding tractable for casual players and heightens the impact of every card. Locations randomize the playing field, so memorization helps less than adaptability. Second Dinner's monthly **OTA (over-the-air) balance patches** are a model for live-service TCG balance.

## 5. Disney Lorcana (Ravensburger, 2023)

**Resource system.** **Ink.** Most cards have a swirl symbol indicating they can be played face-down as ink (an inkwell resource). Each turn you may add 1 card to your inkwell. This is a brilliant solution to the MTG mana-screw problem: any inkable card can be "discarded" into resources, so you're never starved if you're willing to spend a card.

**Turn structure.** Beginning, main, end. Within main, you can play cards, **quest** with characters (each quest grants lore equal to the character's lore value), challenge opponent's characters, sing songs, use abilities, and put cards into your inkwell. Characters have **summoning sickness** ("drying ink") on the turn they're played.

**Win condition.** First to **20 lore** wins. Lore is gained by questing.

**Card type taxonomy.** Characters, Actions (incl. Songs which can be "sung" by a character of equal or higher cost in lieu of paying ink), Items, Locations (recent).

**Deck size and construction.** Minimum 60 cards, max 4 of any card, 2 ink colors maximum (out of six).

**What makes it distinctive.** Lorcana threads a needle: it draws on Disney IP for warm flavor while keeping mechanically rigorous TCG design. The questing-for-lore win condition reframes the genre's instinctive "kill the opponent" framing into something more affirmative — you race rather than brawl, though combat still exists as a tempo lever (you can challenge to remove a quester). The ink system is widely cited in design discussion (e.g., on TCG-focused podcasts like *Lorecast* — unverified recall) as the cleanest fix to the mana problem since the genre began.

## 6. Flesh and Blood (Legend Story Studios, 2019)

**Resource system.** **Pitch system.** Every card has both a casting cost (top-left) and a pitch value of 1, 2, or 3 (color-coded as red/yellow/blue, with red being most powerful but 0 pitch, blue being 3 pitch). To pay for a card, you pitch other cards from your hand to the bottom of your deck. Resources are not a separate card type — every card is dual-use.

**Turn structure.** Action phase (play attacks/non-attacks/equipment activations), defense reactions (instants from defender's hand and arsenal), end phase. Hand refills to a hero-specific size at end of turn (typically 4), enforcing per-turn resource budgets. Combat resolves attack-by-attack with reaction windows on each.

**Win condition.** Reduce opposing hero's life to 0. Some heroes start at 20, others 40, depending on archetype.

**Card type taxonomy.** Heroes, Equipment (head/chest/arms/legs), Weapons, Attack actions, Non-attack actions, Attack reactions, Defense reactions, Instants. Each card has a class (Warrior, Wizard, Ranger, etc.) and talent.

**Deck size and construction.** Constructed: hero + equipment + 60 cards (mostly), all matching the hero's class/talent or generic. Singleton in some formats.

**What makes it distinctive.** FaB has a reputation as **"Magic for adults who didn't like Magic anymore"**. Two innovations stand out: the pitch system (no resource screw, no resource flood, every card is mana), and **no-collection-mandatory ladder** — the company has been vocal that competitive play should not require buying singles. Game flow is heavily attack-defense interactive, more like a fighting game than a Solitaire-with-counterspells experience. James White's design talks at GAMA and similar conventions discuss this (unverified URL recall: fabtcg.com/articles).

## 7. Slay the Spire / Dominion — Deckbuilders

These are not strictly TCGs (no booster-pack collection) but they are essential reference points because they pioneered **deck construction during play**.

**Dominion (Donald X. Vaccarino, 2008).** All players start with the same 10-card deck (7 Coppers + 3 Estates). On your turn: play Action(s), then Buy phase, where you spend the treasure in your hand to buy from a shared "supply." Bought cards go to your discard. When your draw deck empties, you shuffle your discard and continue. **Win condition**: most Victory Points after the supply triggers depletion. Resource system is the **money you draw in hand each turn**, so deck composition is itself the engine.

**Slay the Spire (Mega Crit, 2017–2019).** Single-player roguelike deckbuilder. You start with a small deck, climb a procedurally generated map, fight enemies in card-driven battles, and after each battle pick 1 of 3 reward cards (or skip). Resource is **energy** (3 per turn typically). Cards exhaust to discard; deck reshuffles when empty during a fight. Win condition: reach the top of the spire and beat the boss.

**What makes them distinctive.** Both designs externalize the deckbuilding metagame *into* the game. There's no pre-game construction — you build *as* you play. This shrinks the onboarding cost dramatically (no collection, no netdecking, every game starts even). Dominion's "kingdom" of 10 randomly chosen action piles per game gives infinite variety from a finite card pool. Slay the Spire popularized this for solo digital play and is the genre touchstone for any solo-mode TCG design.

## 8. Star Realms / Hero Realms (White Wizard Games, 2014/2016)

**Resource system.** Trade (the buying currency) and Combat (damage). Both come from cards in your hand each turn. Each turn you draw 5 cards, play them all (no holding), buy/attack, then discard everything and draw 5 more.

**Turn structure.** Main phase (play all cards, activate any synergies, buy from market, attack opponent), discard, draw 5. No interrupt windows — strictly sequential.

**Win condition.** Reduce opponent's "Authority" from 50 to 0. (Hero Realms: Health from 50 to 0.)

**Card type taxonomy.** Ships and Bases (Star Realms); Champions, Actions, Items (Hero Realms). Four factions, each with synergy effects when you play 2+ cards of the same faction in a turn.

**Deck size and construction.** No deck construction — both players start with identical 10-card starter decks (8 Scouts, 2 Vipers). The game itself is the construction phase, drawing from a shared 5-card market that refills from a central trade row.

**What makes it distinctive.** Star Realms is the cleanest example of a **shared-market deckbuilder as a 2-player duel**. Games run 15-20 minutes. The faction-synergy bonus (play a second card of the same faction → bonus effect) gives players a steering mechanism for their deck mid-game without overwhelming choice. Often cited as a gateway TCG-adjacent game for newcomers because zero collection is required.

## 9. Sorcery: Contested Realm (Erik Olofsson, 2024)

**Resource system.** **Mana sites.** Each player has a separate "atlas" deck of land/site cards, drawn from independently. You play sites onto a shared **4×5 grid** (the realm). Sites generate mana of their element (Earth, Air, Fire, Water, plus Void) by being tapped, and units must occupy or move through sites that match their requirements.

**Turn structure.** Begin (draw, recover units), main (play sites, summon units, cast spells, move and attack), end. Units have movement on the grid like a tactics game.

**Win condition.** Reduce opponent's avatar to 0 life. The avatar starts at a particular site and can be threatened directly by attacking units that reach it.

**Card type taxonomy.** Avatar (the hero), Sites, Minions, Magic spells, Aura spells, Artifacts.

**Deck size and construction.** Two decks: a **spellbook** (~50 cards) and an **atlas** (~30 sites). Element-locked construction.

**What makes it distinctive.** Sorcery is the **spatial TCG**. Position on the 4×5 grid matters: line of sight, adjacency, terrain elements. It draws on early-90s fantasy art aesthetics deliberately (the Pat Lawlor / Ian Miller MTG-Alpha mood) and was heavily marketed to Magic players who missed that vibe. Mechanically, it imports tactical-board-game thinking (think *Summoner Wars*) into a TCG frame.

## 10. Yu-Gi-Oh! (Konami, 1999)

**Resource system.** **None.** There is no mana, no energy, no ink, no pitch. You can play any number of cards per turn, limited only by once-per-turn restrictions, summons-per-turn caps (Normal Summon: 1 per turn), and card text.

**Turn structure.** Draw, Standby, Main 1, Battle, Main 2, End. No mana phases. Players have explicit **chain** windows where Spell/Trap effects (and quick-effect monsters) can be activated in response, resolving last-in-first-out — analogous to MTG's stack but called a Chain, with priority rules of its own.

**Win condition.** Reduce opponent's Life Points (starts at 8000) to 0, deck-out, or special "alternative win condition" cards (Exodia: assemble 5 specific cards in hand; Final Countdown).

**Card type taxonomy.** Monsters (Normal, Effect, Ritual, Fusion, Synchro, Xyz, Pendulum, Link), Spells (Normal, Continuous, Equip, Field, Quick-Play, Ritual), Traps (Normal, Continuous, Counter).

**Deck size and construction.** Main Deck 40-60 cards. Extra Deck up to 15 (for Fusion/Synchro/Xyz/Link monsters). Side Deck up to 15 for tournament best-of-three. Max 3 of any card (modified by Forbidden/Limited list).

**What makes it distinctive.** The **no-resource design** is the foundational difference. Combined with extra-deck "summon from a separate pile" mechanics, Yu-Gi-Oh! has evolved into a game of **first-turn 5-minute combos** where one player builds a board and locks the opponent before they ever play. The genre case study in **power creep**: every new mechanic (Synchro 2008, Xyz 2011, Pendulum 2014, Link 2017) was introduced partly to refresh metagame and outpace older archetypes. Forbidden/Limited list updates roughly quarterly are the primary balance tool.

## 11. Netrunner / Android: Netrunner (Richard Garfield/FFG, 1996/2012, Project NISEI fan-made 2018+)

**Resource system.** **Credits** (universal currency, earned via "Gain credit" actions or card effects) and **Clicks** (action points — 4 per turn typically for the Runner, 3 for the Corp). Both sides have their own card pool.

**Turn structure.** Each side spends clicks to draw, gain credit, install/play cards, advance agendas (Corp), or run servers (Runner). Turn ends when clicks are spent. Many cards have paid abilities playable on either turn.

**Win condition.** Score 7 agenda points. Corp scores by advancing and "scoring" agenda cards from servers; Runner scores by stealing them via successful runs. Alternate: Runner is "flatlined" (taking damage when their hand is empty), or Corp is decked.

**Card type taxonomy.** Strongly **asymmetric**. Corp: Agenda, Asset, Operation, Upgrade, ICE (defense). Runner: Event, Hardware, Resource, Program (incl. Icebreakers).

**Deck size and construction.** 45-card minimum (Runner) or identity-influenced (Corp: agenda density requirement scales with deck size). Identity card determines faction influence and deck-size minimum. Influence system limits cross-faction cards.

**What makes it distinctive.** Netrunner is the genre's **asymmetric** game. The two sides are not just thematically different — they don't play remotely the same game. The Corp plays a hidden-information game (face-down cards in servers); the Runner plays an information-poker game (deciding when to run on what). Designer Richard Garfield (who also designed MTG) explicitly cited bluffing as the central design value. After Fantasy Flight lost the license, the **Project NISEI** community continued the game with free print-and-play sets — a precedent for non-commercial TCG longevity. Asymmetric design is rare in TCGs because it doubles development cost, but produces distinctive matchup feel.
