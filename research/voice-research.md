# Dad TCG — Voice & Storytelling Research

*Date: 2026-05-08. Source: Explore agent verb-mining of Masculinity-Detox repo + training-data on Brené Brown / Adam Grant / bell hooks / Mr. Rogers tradition + dad-joke and Sunday-comics structural patterns.*

This is the canonical reference for **voice, vocabulary, and storytelling structure** when writing Dad TCG card text, flavor lines, Year card flavor, and Highlight Reel copy. Every card should pass two informal tests: (1) the **Rogers test** — could this be said to a child without flinching? (2) the **Rapid Reflection test** — does it have lived specificity rather than abstract virtue?

## 2. Mr. Rogers register — the secret unifier

The Rogers cadence: short declarative sentences, 2nd-person, no metaphor stretched past one beat, no mockery, listener presumed worthy before the sentence starts.

**Phrases:**
1. "I like you just the way you are."
2. "Look for the helpers."
3. "Anything mentionable is manageable." / "What is mentionable is manageable."
4. "Won't you be my neighbor?"
5. "You've made this day a special day, by just your being you."
6. "There's no person in the whole world like you."
7. "Feelings are mentionable, and human, and all right to have."
8. "Doing the best we can may still fall short of what we'd like — and that's the most we should ever expect of ourselves."
9. "It's such a good feeling to know you're alive."
10. "If you could only sense how important you are…"
11. Rule of three with repetition: "There are three ways to ultimate success: be kind. Be kind. Be kind."

**Sentence-shape patterns to borrow without naming Rogers:**
- *Imperative + simple object*: "Tell them you're proud."
- *I + verb + you + as-you-are*: "I'm glad you're here."
- *What is X is Y* (paired words, often near-rhyme)
- Direct address with no rhetorical distance — never "the dad," always "you."

> **The Rogers Test:** every card should be sayable to a child without flinching. If it can't be, it's wrong-toned for Dad TCG.

## 3. Dad-joke and Sunday-comics structural patterns

### Dad-joke rules
1. **Literal mishearing of a figurative phrase.** "I'm starving." / "Hi Starving, I'm Dad."
2. **Question that sounds like wisdom, lands as pun.** "Why don't scientists trust atoms? They make up everything."
3. **Punchline arrives unsolicited; teller commits anyway.**
4. **Repetition over decades.** Same joke, same grocery store, same kid groan.
5. **The teller laughs first.** Joy-of-telling precedes joy-of-hearing.
6. **Anti-climactic punchline.** Weak punchline = correct punchline. Strong = trying too hard.

### Sunday-comics grammar
7. **Small domestic event, mythic register.** A leaf-pile is "a dragon's nest."
8. **Kid is the straight man, dad bumbles with heart.** Dad wrong about the cereal brand, right about the hug.
9. **Three-panel rhythm: setup / complication / quiet payoff.** *"Burned the first pancake. Burned the second one. Got the third one right."*
10. **Object-as-character.** The dishwasher, the dented station wagon, the lawn mower — named like friends.
11. **Punch is a feeling, not a joke.** Last panel: kid leans on dad, no dialogue.
12. **Seasonal mundane elevated to cosmic.** Raking leaves → reckoning with mortality.
13. **Repetition-with-variation.** Same beach trip, every summer, slightly different kid.
14. **Kid's question the dad can't answer.** Small philosophy from a small mouth. Good move: "I don't know. What do *you* think?"

## 5. Eighteen positive father modes (for archetype seeds & Year card prompts)

Lived-specific, no named characters. Each captures a single move repeated many times.

1. **The dad who narrates the everyday like color commentary.** "And here he comes, eating a bagel, ladies and gentlemen—" Kid pretends to hate it. Kid is grinning.
2. **The dad who learned to braid hair from YouTube** and isn't great at it but tries every single morning. First-day-of-school braid is uneven. Kid wears it anyway.
3. **The dad who says "tell me what you mean by that"** before reacting. A pause baked into his voice.
4. **The dad with a specific weird hobby the kids reluctantly accept.** Sourdough. Birding. Vintage tools. He talks about it too long. It is the best part of dinner.
5. **The stepfather who waits to be invited.** Doesn't push the title. Drives to the recital anyway. Sits two rows behind in case it's needed.
6. **The single dad who packs lunch the night before.** Three sandwiches at 10pm. A note in the napkin. Same joke every Tuesday.
7. **The granddad who has a uniform.** Same flannel, same hat. Smells like a specific soap. Picks up the phone on the second ring.
8. **The queer dad** who taught the kid that *family* is built, not assigned. No defensiveness. Just the fact, repeated until it lands as ordinary.
9. **The adoptive dad** who keeps the origin story honest. Tells it the same way every year. The kid gets to add lines.
10. **The dad who texts his adult kid a song lyric, no context, three times a week.** Receives no reply. Texts the next one anyway.
11. **The big-brother-figure who became a dad through showing up.** Not biological. Not legal. Picks up at the airport. Knows the password to the Wi-Fi by heart.
12. **The dad who keeps a small notebook of things the kids said.** Writes the bad jokes verbatim. Reads them at graduation.
13. **The dad who fixes the wrong thing first** and the right thing eventually. Replaces the wrong washer. Floods the basement. Laughs first. Then fixes it.
14. **The dad who texts his friends about being a dad.** "Did your kid ever do this?" Three replies. Saves the screenshot.
15. **The mentor-dad / coach-dad who never raises his voice at the field.** Calm cadence, kid's name said like a small bell. The other parents notice.
16. **The dad who shows up to the same coffee shop with the kid every Saturday for 15 years.** Same order. Same booth.
17. **The dad who learned the kid's pronouns and the kid's friends' pronouns** and then the kid's friends' parents' pronouns. Practices in the car. Doesn't make it a thing.
18. **The dad who makes the family playlist and sneaks one of his songs in.** Kids skip it. He's not mad. He'll sneak it again.

## 6. Voice direction (agent's synthesis)

Four overarching guides:

1. **The Rapid-Reflection cadence is the secret weapon.** "You know that thing where… Yeah, when that happens…" works as a Year-card and Moment shape — long compound clause naming a familiar dad-fail, then a short imperative. Solves sincere-but-not-saccharine because the run-on carries comedic specificity (rice-cement, screw-in-tire, lunch-forgotten) while the punchline is moral without being preachy.

2. **The bumbler-with-accountability is the most distinctive voice asset.** The "I forgot lunch" piece works because the narrator is the antagonist of his own story but never wallows. Sheepish, accountable, still-laughing. Anchor this in **Resilience** and **Integrity** factions especially.

3. **bell hooks for archetype seeds, Brown for sentence-shapes, Grant for plain phrases.** The hooks vocabulary is too pointed for casual card flavor; use as concept-seeds (the "will-to-change" dad). Brown's sentence-shapes ("the story I'm telling myself") and Grant's plain phrases ("rethinking," "task conflict not relationship conflict") work as visible card text.

4. **The Rogers register is the unifier.** Two-clause sentences, second person, no irony tax. Every card should pass the test — *could this be said to a child without flinching?* The set holds together tonally even when individual cards lean comic or specific.
